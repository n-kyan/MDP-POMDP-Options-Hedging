      Develops a model where an investor possessing private information about future volatility trades an option, explicitly linking net options order flows (a proxy for order flow imbalance) to the resulting level and curvature of the smile in implied volatilities and Black-Scholes mispricing.

      - **Order Flow Signals Volatility:** Finds that higher net options order flows act as a signal to the market maker that future volatility is expected to be higher, causing the market maker to increase the option price.
      - **Information Asymmetry Model:** Shows that options order flows contain information about future volatility, enabling the market maker to infer the level of future volatility by observing these flows.

      Examines the relationship between trading volume, order flow, and volatility in the Foreign Exchange (FX) market microstructure using a Markov switching approach, which captures asymmetry and regime shifts.

      - **Utilizes Markov Switching GARCH Models:** Finds that the Markov Switching Exponential GARCH (MSEGARCH) model, which accounts for regime switching, time variation, and asymmetry in conditional variance, fits the intraday data better than GARCH and other Markov Switching GARCH models.

      ### **[HTML]** [The impact of option hedging on the spot market volatility](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261560622000304)

      B Anderegg, F Ulmann, [D Sornette](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=HGsSmMAAAAAJ&hl=en&oi=sra) - Journal of International Money and …, 2022 - Elsevier

      Theoretically models the dependence of spot market volatility on the gamma exposure of option market makers (OMMs) and option market takers (OMTs), which is related to their net delta hedge activity (a form of order flow).

      - **Validates Gamma Impact:** Empirically validates that the negative gamma exposure of OMMs significantly increases spot market volatility, providing evidence for a feedback effect where hedging activity influences market dynamics.
      - **Examines Asymmetric Hedging:** Examines how asymmetric net delta hedge demand, arising from OMMs dynamically hedging and OMTs not, leads to changes (increase or decrease) in spot market volatility, suggesting that differential hedging behavior (a form of fill asymmetry) is a volatility factor.
